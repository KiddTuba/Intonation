Demystifying Intonation

A Band Director's Guide to Playing in Tune, Every Note, Every Phrase.

The "Single Note" Trap

Most directors are trained to have their ensemble tune to ONE note (usually Concert Bb or F) at the start of rehearsal, and then immediately proceed to playing the repertoire. While establishing a baseline pitch center is important, this method frequently leads to frustration when festival judges inevitably comment: "Intonation is off."

Why does this happen? Because tuning Concert Bb does not mean the ensemble is "in tune." It means one note on one instrument is aligned to a standard frequency.

In reality, every interval has its problems, and every instrument has its built-in flaws. True ensemble intonation requires understanding the harmonic function of the note within a chord (Just Intonation) combined with knowledge of the physical tendencies of the instrument playing it.

Just Intonation vs. Equal Temperament

Electronic tuners use Equal Temperament, dividing the octave into 12 perfectly equal half-steps. However, physics and the harmonic series dictate that perfectly equal half-steps do not create perfectly resonant, beatless chords.

When playing wind instruments, we must adjust pitches dynamically to match Just Intonation—tuning intervals precisely to the root of the chord being played.

Chord Factor Cents Adjustment Physical Fix Action
Root / Octaves 0 cents Tune accurately to pitch center.
Major 3rd -14 cents Lower the pitch significantly
Minor 3rd +16 cents Raise the pitch significantly
Perfect 5th +2 cents Raise the pitch slightly
Minor 7th (Dom Chord) -31 cents Lower the pitch extremely

The "Moving Target" of Long Tones

A single note by itself might be perfectly in tune with a tuner, but it can quickly become out of tune when the harmony changes around it.

Attention Low Winds & Bass Voices: It is easy to "fall asleep" on a long, sustained pedal point. However, if you are sustaining a Concert Bb while the band moves from a Bb Major chord (where you act as the Root) to a Gb Major chord (where your Bb acts as the Major 3rd), your pitch must dynamically lower by 14 cents without you ever changing your fingering or the written note!

This is why performers must listen actively and continually adjust. Use the audio playback tools in the Analyzer Tab to let students hear these shifts in isolation, training their ears to lock into the beatless center of the chord.

Instrument Tendencies & Marking the Score

You cannot just tell a flutist to "lower the third." If their written note is E6 (top space), that note on a flute is naturally flat. If they try to lip down an already flat note to satisfy a -14 cent requirement, the chord will sound worse. You must combine chord theory with instrument design. Equip your performers with visual reminders on their sheet music mapping the PHYSICAL FIX needed.

Down Arrow (↓)

The physical fix is to lower the pitch. Mark this for Major 3rds, Dominant 7ths, or whenever an instrument tends to play a note inherently sharp.

Up Arrow (↑)

The physical fix is to raise the pitch. Mark this for Minor 3rds, Perfect 5ths, or whenever an instrument tends to play a note inherently flat.

Action Step: Input an ensemble passage into the Analyzer. Look at the "Score Mark" column. Tell your students to grab their pencils, locate the chord, and draw that exact arrow over their note. Have them play the chord before adjusting, listen to the "beats," then implement the fix arrow to lock it in.
